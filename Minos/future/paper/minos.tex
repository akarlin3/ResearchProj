\begin{abstract}
A reported uncertainty is usually judged by \emph{coverage}: does the stated interval contain the
truth at its nominal rate? But an uncertainty estimate is used to \emph{make a decision}, and we ask
a different question --- what is the error bar \emph{worth}, and when should it be \emph{distrusted}?
We give two analytic answers. First, statistical and decision calibration are different objectives
that diverge under a misspecified (skewed) posterior: the decision-optimal error-bar scale departs
from the coverage-optimal one by a \emph{decision--calibration gap} $G=(1/6)\,|z^*(\lambda)|\,\gamma$
to leading order in the posterior skewness $\gamma$, with a coefficient that is a pure
decision-boundary quantity (Theorem~\ref{thm:gap}). Second, a label-free monitor that watches for a
stale calibration recovers the \emph{observable} component of deployment drift (bounded-detectable,
$\mathrm{AUC}\to1$) but is provably blind to a \emph{hidden} component that induces the same regret
($\mathrm{AUC}=1/2$, Theorem~\ref{thm:floor}) --- a principled limit, and the case for labeled
spot-checks. We then apply both to intravoxel incoherent motion (IVIM) diffusion MRI. On
Fashion-calibrated posteriors\PROV{} the decision--calibration gap is real and clinically material,
though dominated by the reported bar's own miscalibration rather than the small-skew law; the
label-free monitor\PROV{} fires on observable shifts ($\mathrm{AUC}$ up to \numSnrAUC) and is blind
to the high-$D^*$ identifiability wall ($\mathrm{AUC}=\numHiddenTestAUC$), exactly the
observable/hidden split.
\end{abstract}

% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
Quantitative imaging increasingly reports a per-voxel uncertainty alongside each parameter estimate.
The dominant question asked of that uncertainty is \emph{statistical calibration}: a nominal-$L$
interval should contain the truth a fraction $L$ of the time. Calibration is necessary, but it is not
the question a \emph{user} asks. An error bar is consumed by a decision --- treat, spare, escalate
--- and a decision has an asymmetric cost. This paper asks two questions about the \emph{decision
value} of a reported uncertainty:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{When is calibrating for coverage the wrong objective for the decision?} We show the two
objectives diverge under posterior skew, by a gap we compute in closed form.
\item \textbf{When should a deployed error bar be distrusted, using no labels?} We show label-free
monitoring has a hard floor: it cannot see a hidden channel of drift that nonetheless changes the
decision.
\end{enumerate}
Both answers are first proved in an idealized model and then applied to IVIM, where the perfusion
parameters ($D^*$, $f$) are notoriously hard to estimate and where calibrated uncertainty is the
object of active study.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Positioning}\label{sec:pos}
Minos does not propose a new calibration method; it prices and bounds existing ones. The relevant
\emph{baselines} are: \textbf{loss-calibrated Bayesian inference}~\cite{lacostejulien2011,vadera2021},
which tunes a posterior so the \emph{decision} is optimal; \textbf{decision
calibration}~\cite{zhao2021}, which asks a predictor's probabilities to be calibrated for a class of
decisions; and \textbf{decision curve analysis} (DCA)~\cite{vickers2006}, which prices a single
threshold's net benefit. Each targets one object. Minos instead computes \emph{how far} the
decision-optimal scale departs from the coverage-optimal one (the gap scaling law --- a quantity
these lines do not produce, since each targets a single calibrated object), and proves the
\emph{detectability limit} of monitoring such a correction for staleness. The OOD /
distribution-shift-under-uncertainty literature~\cite{ovadia2019} studies calibration under shift
empirically; Theorem~\ref{thm:floor} adds the label-free detectability floor it does not state.

\section{The model}\label{sec:model}
We use the posterior-centric model locked against the Minos-Core simulation code. The report centre
is $\mu$ and the truth is $\theta=\mu+s\,u$, where $u$ is a zero-mean, unit-variance skew-normal
error of standardized third cumulant $\gamma$; to leading order the true per-report posterior is the
Gram--Charlier expansion $p(\theta\mid\mu)=\tfrac1s\varphi\!\big(\tfrac{\theta-\mu}{s}\big)
[1+\tfrac{\gamma}{6}\mathrm{He}_3(\tfrac{\theta-\mu}{s})]$. The reported posterior is the Gaussian
$\mathcal N(\mu,(\tau s)^2)$; $\tau$ is the calibration knob. The piecewise-linear utility has a
single active threshold $t_2$ (treat/escalate), with under:over cost asymmetry
$\lambda=k_{\mathrm under}/k_{\mathrm over}\ge1$. We write $\tau_{\mathrm{stat}}$ for the
coverage-optimal scale and $\tau^*$ for the decision-optimal scale; the gap is $G=\tau^*-\tau_{\mathrm{stat}}$.

\begin{assumption}[regime]\label{ass:regime}
Expansions are leading-order in $\gamma$ (neglected terms $O(\gamma^{4/3})$, $O(\gamma^2)$); a single
active threshold; bounded Lipschitz utility ($\mathrm{Lip}_\theta U=k_{\mathrm under}=\numLU$);
$\tau^*$ identified only where the expected-utility curve is non-flat.
\end{assumption}

\section{Theorem 1 --- the decision--calibration gap}\label{sec:thy1}
\begin{theorem}[gap scaling law]\label{thm:gap}
Under the locked model, to leading order in the posterior skewness $\gamma$,
\[
\tau_{\mathrm{stat}}(\gamma)=1-a\gamma+O(\gamma^2),\ a=0;\qquad
\tau^*(\gamma)=1+\tfrac16|z^*(\lambda)|\,\gamma+O(\gamma^{4/3});
\]
\[
G(\gamma)=\tau^*-\tau_{\mathrm{stat}}=\tfrac16\,|z^*(\lambda)|\,\gamma+O(\gamma^{4/3}),
\]
where $z^*(\lambda)<0$ solves $(\lambda-1)\psi(z)+z=0$ with $\psi(z)=z\Phi(z)+\varphi(z)$.
\end{theorem}
The coverage scale is \emph{first-order skew-insensitive} ($a=0$, the odd Gram--Charlier correction
integrates to zero over a symmetric interval), so the entire first-order gap is decision-side; the
coefficient is purely the decision-boundary offset $z^*(\lambda)$ and does not depend on where the
population sits relative to the threshold. All coefficients are sympy-derived (theory GATE~1). At the
default cell ($\lambda=3$, $\gamma=0.667$) the leading-order $\tau^*=\numTauStarLead$ and the full
gap $G=\numGfull$ ($z^*(3)=\numZstarThree$, slope $|z^*(3)|/6=\numGleadSlope$); the theory reproduces
the published v2/v3 sweeps to within tolerance with no fitted constant (GATE~3).

\section{Theorem 2 --- the label-free detectability floor}\label{sec:thy2}
\begin{theorem}[detectability floor]\label{thm:floor}
Split deployment staleness into an \emph{observable} channel (the reported point law shifts) and a
\emph{hidden} channel (only the truth shifts). The observable stale-correction regret is
bounded-detectable, $R_{\mathrm obs}\le L_U\cdot W_1(P_O^{\mathrm dep},P_O^{\mathrm cal})$ with
$L_U=k_{\mathrm under}=\numLU$ (theory GATE~2). The hidden channel leaves the observable law exactly
invariant, so every label-free monitor $M=f(\{\mu\})$ has identical law under fresh and stale
deployment: its detection $\mathrm{AUC}=\numHiddenAUC$ exactly.
\end{theorem}
The observable channel tracks regret and is detectable ($\mathrm{AUC}=\numObsAUC$); the hidden channel
sits at the information-theoretic floor ($\mathrm{AUC}=\numHiddenAUC$). The monitor is therefore not
``imperfect'' --- it is optimal up to a floor no label-free statistic can cross. The only way to see
the hidden channel is to read a label: the case for labeled repeatability spot-checks (\S\ref{sec:thru}).

\section{Applied I --- the gap on Fashion-calibrated IVIM posteriors}\label{sec:cp2}
\provnote{this entire section consumes Fashion's calibrated posteriors.}
We apply the gap to a clean synthetic IVIM cohort (Fashion's simulator, pancreatic-anchor priors;
no clinical data) with Fashion-calibrated uncertainty. For the perfusion parameters we compute, on
the real $(\theta,\mu,\sigma)$, the coverage scale $\tau_{\mathrm{stat}}$, the decision scale
$\tau^*$, and the gap, reusing the validated Minos decision core.

\paragraph{The qualitative claim holds.} Coverage-calibration is \emph{not} decision-calibration on
real Fashion-calibrated posteriors: a gap exists and using the reported bar for the decision leaves
substantial regret (up to $\numMaxRegret$ utility units for $D^*$)\PROV{}.

\paragraph{The quantitative law does not transfer --- reported, not tuned.} The reported $\sigma$ is
itself miscalibrated: mean $\tau_{\mathrm{stat}}=\numMeanTauStat$ and mean $\mathrm{std}(u)=\numMeanStdU$
(the bar \emph{under-disperses} the $D^*$/$f$ error, reproducing Fashion's $D^*$ under-coverage and
extending it to $f$)\PROV{}. Even after coverage-calibration, the residual decision offset exceeds the
leading-order law by a median factor of $\numGstdRatio\times$ across the skewed cells\PROV{}; signs
agree in $\numSignOk/\numNAsym$ cells, but a $\numGstdRatio\times$ magnitude gap is not quantitative
support. The cause is honest model mismatch: real IVIM errors are biased ($\mathrm E[u]\neq0$),
over-dispersed, and heavy-tailed, and the measured skew is generator-dependent --- deviations the
small-skew, zero-mean, unit-variance idealization abstracts away. The theory is the idealized account;
the applied gap is the empirical, miscalibration-dominated reality.

\section{The observable/hidden throughline}\label{sec:thru}
The same split recurs across the program. \textbf{Gauge} shows the high-$D^*$ conditional-coverage gap
is an irreducible identifiability wall (the Cram\'er--Rao bound on $D^*$ exceeds the latent-regime
width), the empirical instance of Theorem~\ref{thm:floor}'s hidden channel. \textbf{Echo} reads sparse
labels (repeatability spot-checks) --- the only instrument that can cross the label-free floor. Minos
supplies the theory that ties them: a cheap label-free monitor is provably optimal up to a floor, and
a sparse labeled check is the principled complement, not a redundancy.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Discussion and scope}\label{sec:disc}
Both theory results are leading-order / idealized (Assumption~\ref{ass:regime}); Theorem~\ref{thm:floor}(i)
assumes the hidden channel leaves observables exactly invariant. The applied sections show the
\emph{mechanisms} survive contact with real IVIM --- a decision-vs-coverage gap, an observable/hidden
detectability split --- while the clean scaling law is an idealization that biased, over-dispersed
IVIM posteriors do not satisfy quantitatively. We report this honestly rather than tune it: the value
of the theory is that it names the right objects (a gap; a floor) and predicts their existence and
sign, which the applied data confirm.
