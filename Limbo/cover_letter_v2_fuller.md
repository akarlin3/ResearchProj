Dear Editors,

I am pleased to submit "Trustworthy uncertainty quantification for quantitative and
diffusion body MRI, and its decision-use in MR-guided adaptive radiotherapy: a field
review" for consideration as a Review article in *Physics and Imaging in Radiation
Oncology*.

The MR-linac has made it feasible to re-optimize a treatment plan from the day's anatomy,
and the field is now moving toward steering dose from the day's biology: dose painting
from a biological target volume, and response-adaptive changes driven by diffusion or
perfusion biomarkers. Early clinical translation is real but uneven — a randomized
intraprostatic focal boost improved biochemical control, dose-painting-by-numbers has been
delivered in early-phase studies, yet a phase III PET-guided dose-redistribution trial
showed no overall benefit. Across these efforts the unsolved link is the same: a biomarker
reaches the plan not as a value but as a number with an error bar, and we rarely know
whether that error bar is honest and stable enough to justify changing a dose.

This review surveys that link. It reads the literature as the path a biomarker error bar
must travel to reach a dose decision — trust (is the bar calibrated and covering?), value
(does a trusted bar change the decision?), and action (what happens when it drives dose on
an MR-linac?) — and synthesizes work that is otherwise scattered across imaging-physics,
calibration, decision-theory, and MR-guided-RT communities that seldom cite one another.
The central empirical finding is that trust is conditional: broadly defensible for the
diffusion coefficient and ADC, but fragile for the perfusion parameters, where measured
test–retest reliability is poor — including a non-trivial repeatability floor for ADC
measured on the MR-linac itself. The review closes with four open problems for
biomarker-driven adaptive RT: conditional coverage in the regime where it fails; the step
from calibration to the dose decision; end-to-end propagation of biomarker uncertainty
into dose; and standards that make uncertainty comparable across estimators, extending
QIBA metrology.

The synthesis is aimed at the radiation-oncology-physics readership that must make these
biomarker-to-dose judgments, which is why phiRO — at the intersection of physics and
imaging for radiation oncology — is its natural home. It does not advocate a single
method; it offers that readership a defensible map of what is and is not yet established,
and an explicit agenda of what to solve next. I make no claim to completeness: this is a
single-author narrative synthesis, not a systematic review, and I say so plainly in the
manuscript.
